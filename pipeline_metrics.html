Building a Revenue Attribution for Pipeline Tracking and Capacity Planning

During my time at Github and Vercel, we built a last-touch attribution model that changed how we allocated marketing spend, structured our sales team, and made strategic decisions. Below are some key learnigns.

The shift started when we asked the question: where did that pipeline actually come from, so that we can double down on what works?

Collectively answering that question created a tight(er) alignment between Sales and Marketing.
During the attribution building process, what mattered most was the strategic conversation where both teams agreed on what campaigns get prioritized, what lookback window the business is comfortable with (GitHub had a much longer window than Vercel), and which touchpoints actually matter for revenue generation.

The calculations became an operational representation of those strategic decisions, not the other way around.

Key learnings:

  • Sales needs to be deeply involved because they have the nuanced understanding of deal flow, customer behavior, and market reality that the numbers should reflect.
  • The attribution model should be flexible enough to evolve based on what Sales is seeing in the field, because if the data doesn't align with their lived experience of where deals actually come from, the whole system becomes irrelevant.
  • Set goals according to seasonality. Then turn on the dial on what works keeping in mind staffing
  • Weighted Pipeline and Win rate will help you understand where to invest when time and/or resources are a constraint

The Value of attribution by GTM Motions

GTM motions was a major pivot from traditional attribution breakdown by Marketing versus Sales. My time implemeting PQL at Github taught me that tiger teams are a real powerhouse for hyper-growth and rarely you will find a GTM play that consists of a single team working on it.

That is why at Vercel we looked at the GTM Motions primarily not the teams

Most GTM motions will have several people from different teams. Here is a simple daily use case for you to consider: Field Marketers organizing an event, Demand Gen doubling down with a Webinar that partners with Sales Eng, GTM OPs scoring the lead, and handing off to an SDR that qualifies the Opp.

GTM Motion pipeline implementation:

  • Map the GTM Motions the business will utilize in the quarter
  • Calculate the historical pipeline and win rate for each GTM Motion - 1-2 years is fine.
  • Calculate the % that each GTM Motion contributed to Total Pipeline per quarter
  • Assign each a pipeline goal based on historical data + projected growth - respect the quarter information to keep goals realistic
  • Have someone or a team responsible for the OKR

How last-touch attribution works

Last-touch attribution tracks the final interaction that led to opportunity creation. It follows a simple hierarchy:
[below I am assuming a breakdown based on what we had at Vercel. Team structure and motions will vary]

Attribution Hierarchy (60-day window)
  • Partner: Referrals from integration partners or resellers
  • Customer Success: Expansion opportunities identified by CS team
  • Sales Outbound: Direct outreach activities (emails, calls, LinkedIn)
  • Marketing Touch: Events, content downloads, webinars, ads
  • Product: Trial sign-ups, in-product conversion flows

The key insight: not all pipeline is created equal. A $50K deal from a warm inbound lead has different cost and time efficiencies implications than the same deal from cold outbound. The source tells you what to scale.

Calculations blueprint

Understanding the math behind attribution metrics ensures consistent measurement and accurate analysis. Here's how each metric in the dashboard is calculated:

Pipeline Source Metrics
  • Pipeline Amount: Sum of all opportunity amounts where last_touch_source = [source_type]
  • % of Total Pipeline: (Source Pipeline Amount ÷ Total Pipeline Amount) × 100
  • % vs Goal: (Actual Source Pipeline ÷ Source Target) × 100
  • Avg Deal Size: Source Pipeline Amount ÷ Number of Opportunities
  • Number of Oppt: COUNT(opportunities) WHERE last_touch_source = [source_type]
Quarter Performance Metrics
  • Generated: SUM(all opportunity amounts) for date range
  • Attainment: (Total Generated ÷ Quarter Target) × 100
  • Opportunities: COUNT(all opportunities) for date range
  • Avg Deal Size: Total Generated ÷ Total Opportunities
Rep Performance Metrics
  • Source Pipeline by Rep: SUM(opportunity amounts) WHERE owner = [rep] AND last_touch_source = [source]
  • Total by Rep: SUM(all source pipeline amounts) for individual rep
  • Top Source: MAX(source pipeline amount) with percentage of rep total
  • Top Source %: (Highest Source Amount ÷ Rep Total) × 100

Resource Constraint Decision Framework:

Let's suppose that we have limited time/budget for Q4. We can use the combination of weight ratio and win rate to look for high ROI investments:

Expansion (90% weight ratio + 45% win rate) = Immediate Priority

    Requires least resources per dollar of reliable pipeline Existing relationships reduce sales cycle and increase predictability Action: Hire 1-2 CSMs with revenue quotas before adding more SDRs

Product PLG (80% weight ratio + 35% win rate) = Scale Quickly

    High conversion with minimal human intervention required 23 day cycles enable rapid iteration and optimization Action: Invest in product conversion flows and trial experience over manual outreach

Inbound (70% weight ratio + 28% win rate) = Steady Investment

    Reliable volume with predictable conversion Lower resource intensity than outbound prospecting Action: Maintain current marketing spend, optimize for quality over quantity and keep current SDRs focused on this motion

Outbound (40% weight ratio + 18% win rate) = Last Priorit

    Highest resource requirement for lowest predictable return 67-day cycles tie up rep capacity longest Action: Only invest after optimizing higher-ROI sources

The Resource Math:

With constrained budgets, expansion delivers $252K weighted pipeline from just 15 opportunities (45% win rate), while outbound requires 41 opportunities to generate $358K weighted pipeline (18% win rate). The efficiency difference is 3:1 in favor of expansion.

The technical implementation

Building this system requires three components: data collection, attribution logic, and reporting infrastructure.

Example of a Attribution Logic Implementation Flow
How pipeline source attribution worked in practice at Github.
Goal: Track revenue by touch-point for each Github Teams* member that interacted with it
*GitHub Teams are groups of organization members that reflect a given company or group's structure with cascading access permissions and mentions.

The key is consistent data collection across all touchpoints: CRM activities, marketing automation, product analytics, and customer success interactions. Without clean data, attribution becomes guesswork.

Implementation framework

Here is where I started at Vercel:

[ week 1-2 ]
Data Infrastructure and Strategic Aligment
Audited all touchpoint data sources (CRM, marketing automation, product analytics). Implemented consistent UTM tracking, SFDC Campaigns type, and lead source capture. Worked across teams to understand the GTM motions and Tiger Teams. Built initial attribution logic following the hierarchy model.
[ week 3-4 ]
Dashboard Creation
Created pipeline source breakdown views. Built rep-level performance dashboards. Set up automated reporting for leadership team.
[ week 5-8 ]
Optimization
Weekly meeting to analyze source performance trends. Test resource reallocation based on attribution data. Implement feedback loops for continuous improvement.
Implementation takeaways

The full implementation includes System integration, Teams aligment, and rep-level coaching frameworks.

When you know that 40% of your pipeline comes from inbound marketing but only 18% comes from product-led growth, you make different hiring decisions. When you see that your best enterprise rep generates 49% of their pipeline from inbound while another excels at outbound, you coach them differently.
