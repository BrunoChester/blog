Scaling considerations

Most knowledge management systems break at scale. Not from technical limitations, but from organizational ones.

The technical architecture handles growth reasonably well. TF-IDF vectorization scales linearly with document count. The Flask deployment can serve dozens of concurrent users on basic infrastructure. Adding documents means reprocessing the knowledge base, but that's a background operation that takes minutes, not hours.

The real scaling challenge is knowledge quality.

When you have 5 people contributing to your knowledge base, maintaining consistency is straightforward. When you have 50 people across different regions and functions, you get knowledge drift. Sales playbooks written by your East Coast enterprise team don't necessarily translate to your West Coast SMB motion. Competitive intelligence from your security-focused customers might not apply to your developer-focused prospects.

This creates a content governance problem disguised as a technical problem.

Current implementation approach

The system starts with structured Notion databases because structure forces clarity. You can't just dump random documents into the knowledge base and expect coherent responses. Each database has a specific purpose: playbooks, competitive intelligence, case studies, objections, product information.

This constraint is actually valuable. It forces teams to organize their institutional knowledge in ways that make sense for retrieval, not just storage.
